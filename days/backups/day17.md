<h3 id="kyoto-intro">Welcome to Kyoto</h3>

<p>Kyoto served as Japan's imperial capital for over 1,000 years and remains the country's cultural heart. With its 1,600 Buddhist temples, 400 Shinto shrines, magnificent palaces, traditional gardens, and preserved wooden architecture, Kyoto offers a glimpse into Japan's rich heritage and traditions.</p>

<p>Today marks your transition from the modern, energetic city of Osaka to the more traditional atmosphere of Kyoto. After settling into your hotel near Nijo Station, you'll visit one of Japan's most iconic sites - Fushimi Inari Shrine with its thousands of vermilion torii gates creating tunnels that wind up Mount Inari.</p>

   <p>Consider these approaches for the best experience:</p>

     <li><strong>Partial Hike:</strong> Continue past Yotsutsuji intersection for another 15-30 minutes to see additional shrine areas with fewer people.</li>
     <li><strong>Full Loop:</strong> The complete 4km mountain trail takes 2-3 hours and passes through all 10,000+ torii gates. Recommended only if you're energetic and have sufficient time.</li>

   <h4>Cultural Significance</h4>
   <p>Fushimi Inari has been a sacred site since 711 CE. The shrine is dedicated to Inari, the Shinto deity of rice, sake, and prosperity. The thousands of torii gates are actually donations from individuals and businesses seeking good fortune - the donor's name and date of the donation are inscribed on the back of each gate. Fox statues (kitsune) throughout the shrine are considered messengers of Inari and often hold symbolic items in their mouths.</p>

<h3 id="practical-info">Practical Information for Kyoto</h3>

<h4>Transportation in Kyoto</h4>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Subway:</strong> Kyoto has two subway lines - the north-south Karasuma Line (Green) and east-west Tozai Line (Purple)</li>
  <li><strong>Buses:</strong> Extensive bus network covers areas not reached by trains. Day passes available (¥700 for city buses)</li>
  <li><strong>JR Lines:</strong> JR Sagano/San-in Line runs east-west through the city; JR Nara Line runs north-south</li>
  <li><strong>Private Railways:</strong> Keihan, Hankyu, and Kintetsu lines connect various parts of the city and region</li>
  <li><strong>IC Cards:</strong> Your PASMO, Suica, ICOCA or other IC cards work on all public transportation in Kyoto</li>
</ul>

<h4>Useful Japanese Phrases</h4>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Sumimasen</strong> - Excuse me/I'm sorry (useful for getting attention)</li>
  <li><strong>O-mizu o kudasai</strong> - Water please</li>
  <li><strong>Eigo no menu arimasu ka?</strong> - Do you have an English menu?</li>
  <li><strong>Kore o kudasai</strong> - This please (while pointing)</li>
  <li><strong>Ikura desu ka?</strong> - How much is it?</li>
  <li><strong>Oishii desu</strong> - It's delicious</li>
  <li><strong>Arigatō gozaimasu</strong> - Thank you very much</li>
</ul>

<h4>Money & Payment</h4>
<ul>
  <li>Many smaller establishments in Kyoto still prefer cash</li>
  <li>ATMs are available at 7-Eleven, post offices, and major banks</li>
  <li>Credit cards are increasingly accepted at larger restaurants and shops</li>
  <li>IC cards can be used for small purchases at convenience stores and some vending machines</li>
</ul>

<h4>Weather in May</h4>
<p>Kyoto in May typically experiences pleasant spring weather:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Average temperatures: 15-25°C (59-77°F)</li>
  <li>May can have occasional rain showers, so a small umbrella is useful</li>
  <li>Evenings can be cooler, so a light jacket is recommended</li>
</ul>

<h4>Kyoto vs. Osaka: Cultural Differences</h4>
<p>As you transition from Osaka to Kyoto, you might notice some cultural differences:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Kyoto residents are known for being more reserved and formal compared to Osakans</li>
  <li>The pace is generally slower and more deliberate in Kyoto</li>
  <li>Kyoto cuisine tends to be more subtle and refined, highlighting natural flavors</li>
  <li>Traditional customs and etiquette are more strictly observed in Kyoto</li>
</ul>

<h4>Fushimi Inari Shrine Etiquette</h4>
<ul>
  <li>As with all Shinto shrines, bow slightly before passing through torii gates</li>
  <li>Keep to the sides of the paths when taking photos to allow others to pass</li>
  <li>Speak quietly, especially near the main shrine buildings</li>
  <li>If you see an active ceremony or prayer, maintain a respectful distance</li>
  <li>Proper shrine etiquette includes bowing twice, clapping twice, then bowing once more when praying</li>
</ul>
