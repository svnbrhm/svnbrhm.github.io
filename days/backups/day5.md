   <h4>Japanese Food Context</h4>
   <p>Okinawa Soba (沖縄そば) is different from mainland Japanese soba:</p>
   <ul>
     <li>Made from wheat flour (not buckwheat)</li>
     <li>Thicker, chewier noodles similar to udon</li>
     <li>Served in a clear pork-based broth</li>
     <li>Typically topped with stewed pork belly (rafute), fish cake, and green onions</li>
     <li>A designated traditional food of Okinawa Prefecture</li>

     <h4>Japanese Food Context</h4>
     <p>Okinawan cuisine has unique characteristics:</p>
     <ul>
       <li><strong>Goya Champuru (ゴーヤーチャンプルー)</strong>: A stir-fry of bitter melon, tofu, egg, and often pork. A signature Okinawan dish.</li>
       <li><strong>Rafute (ラフテー)</strong>: Tender pork belly simmered in awamori (Okinawan liquor), soy sauce, and brown sugar until meltingly soft.</li>
       <li><strong>Umibudo (海ぶどう)</strong>: "Sea grapes" seaweed with small, bubble-like structures that pop in your mouth. Often served with ponzu sauce.</li>
       <li><strong>Jimami Tofu (ジーマミー豆腐)</strong>: Peanut tofu, a creamy tofu-like dish made from peanuts rather than soybeans.</li>
     </ul>
     <p>Okinawan food reflects the islands' history, with influences from China, Southeast Asia, and America.</p>
