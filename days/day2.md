  <div class="section-content-wrapper">
    <h4>IC-Karten: Pasmo & Suica</h4>
    <p>Für den einfachen Transport in Tokio empfiehlt es sich, eine wiederaufladbare IC-Karte (Pasmo oder Suica) zu erwerben. Diese funktionieren für alle öffentlichen Verkehrsmittel in Tokio und ersparen das Kaufen von Einzeltickets.</p>
    <ul>
      <li><i class="fas fa-info-circle fa-fw"></i> <span class="detail-label">Kauf:</span> <span class="detail-value">An Automaten in jedem Bahnhof (inkl. Flughafenstationen). Grundgebühr ca. ¥500 (wird zurückerstattet, wenn Sie die Karte zurückgeben).</span></li>
      <li><i class="fas fa-yen-sign fa-fw"></i> <span class="detail-label">Aufladung:</span> <span class="detail-value">An den gleichen Automaten mit Bargeld in Schritten von ¥1.000. Empfohlene erste Aufladung: ¥3.000-5.000.</span></li>
      <li><i class="fas fa-subway fa-fw"></i> <span class="detail-label">Nutzung:</span> <span class="detail-value">Einfach auf die Kartenleser an Bahnhoftüren halten (berührungslos). Der Fahrpreis wird automatisch abgebucht.</span></li>
      <li><i class="fas fa-store fa-fw"></i> <span class="detail-label">Weitere Nutzung:</span> <span class="detail-value">Kann auch in vielen Convenience Stores, Automaten und einigen Restaurants verwendet werden.</span></li>
    </ul>
    <h4>Metro vs. JR Lines</h4>
    <p>Tokio hat mehrere Bahnsysteme, die wichtigsten sind:</p>
    <ul>
      <li><i class="fas fa-train fa-fw"></i> <span class="detail-label">Tokyo Metro & Toei Subway:</span> <span class="detail-value">U-Bahn-Netzwerke, die die Stadt abdecken. Die Ginza-Linie (orange) ist für diesen Tag besonders wichtig.</span></li>
      <li><i class="fas fa-train fa-fw"></i> <span class="detail-label">JR Lines:</span> <span class="detail-value">Betreibt die Yamanote-Linie (grün, ringförmig) und andere wichtige Linien, einschließlich Ueno Station.</span></li>
      <li><i class="fas fa-info-circle fa-fw"></i> <span class="detail-label">Navigation:</span> <span class="detail-value">Google Maps oder Japan Travel App funktionieren gut für Routenplanung. Bahnhöfe haben Farbcodes und Nummerierungen, die helfen, sich zu orientieren.</span></li>
    </ul>
    <h4>Tagesspezifische Transporthinweise</h4>
    <p>Für Tag 2 wird hauptsächlich die Ginza-Linie (orange) für die Fahrt zwischen Asakusa und Ueno verwendet. Beide Bereiche können auch gut zu Fuß erkundet werden.</p>

      <h4>History & Cultural Context</h4>
      <p>Senso-ji was founded in 628 CE, making it Tokyo's oldest temple. According to legend, two fishermen found a statue of Kannon (the Buddhist goddess of mercy) in the Sumida River, and even though they returned it to the river, it kept coming back to them. The temple was built to honor this statue, which is now enshrined here but never shown to the public. Senso-ji survived WWII bombings (though the current structures are mostly post-war reconstructions) and remains an active place of worship while also being Tokyo's most visited temple.</p>
      <h4>Temple Etiquette</h4>
      <ul>
        <li>It's acceptable to take photos in most areas, but be respectful during ceremonies</li>
        <li>At the main hall, you may make an offering (small coins), clap twice, bow, and make a wish</li>
        <li>There are incense burners where many visitors wave the smoke toward themselves (believed to have healing properties)</li>
        <li>Don't point with chopsticks at the food stalls as this resembles funeral rituals in Japan</li>
      </ul>

<details>
  <summary><strong>Other Traditional Snacks to Try</strong></summary>
  <div class="details-content-wrapper">
    <h4>Age Manju (揚げ饅頭)</h4>
    <p>Deep-fried buns filled with sweet bean paste. The contrast between the crispy exterior and soft, sweet interior makes these irresistible. Look for freshly fried ones for the best experience.</p>
    
    <h4>Ningyo-yaki (人形焼き)</h4>
    <p>Small, shaped cakes filled with sweet red bean paste. In Asakusa, these are often molded into shapes related to the temple or traditional Japanese designs. They're usually made fresh on-site in special molds.</p>
    
    <h4>Senbei (煎餅)</h4>
    <p>Savory rice crackers, sometimes grilled right in front of you. Available in various flavors, from simple soy sauce to nori (seaweed) or spicy varieties. These crunchy snacks are perfect for munching while walking.</p>
    
    <h4>Where to Find</h4>
    <p>All these treats can be found along Nakamise-dori or in the surrounding shopping streets. Look for shops with lines of locals or where you can see the items being freshly made.</p>

   <h4>About Tonkatsu</h4>
   <p>Tonkatsu is a Japanese dish featuring a thick, panko-breaded and deep-fried pork cutlet. It first appeared in Japan during the Meiji era (1868-1912) as Japanese chefs adapted Western cooking techniques. The dish is typically served with shredded cabbage, rice, miso soup, and tonkatsu sauce (a thick, fruity brown sauce). Many restaurants offer unlimited cabbage refills, which is meant to be eaten with the cutlet for balance. Proper tonkatsu should be crispy outside but juicy inside.</p>
